# Who else is out there — competitor scan

I sat down and opened an account in each of them. These notes are from actually using the products, not from their marketing pages.

## Monday

The biggest in the category, and where our name comes from. Excellent tool — for teams. Everything there revolves around assigning to team members, packed boards, automations. For a freelancer working alone, it's like buying a bus to drive yourself to the corner store. The pricing is built for a team too: you pay per seat, and when you're a single seat it doesn't add up.

Our message against them is simple: Monday is for companies, Tuesday is for the one-person company. We're not trying to be a smaller version of them. We're a different thing.

## Notion

Absurdly flexible, and that's also the problem. Notion is a blank page. You can build anything in it, but you have to build everything. A freelancer who wants to work, not to build themselves a management system from scratch, breaks after two weeks. I've watched plenty of people start in Notion full of enthusiasm and abandon it. With us, tasks are connected to the client and the invoice from the first moment, no assembly required.

Against Notion the argument is: we don't give you tools to build with, we give you the business already built.

## Trello

Simple and pleasant, but shallow. A board with cards, and that's it. There's no concept of clients, of money, of a portal. It's a tool for tasks, not for a business. A freelancer has to wire it up to five other tools to make it work, and then again they're managing tools instead of working.

Against Trello the argument is: we're not another task board, we're the one place where the tasks, the clients, and the money live together.

## Where we're different, in one line

Everyone was built for a team and tried to shrink down to a freelancer, or was built as a blank page and left you to build it yourself. Tuesday was built from day one for one person who is the whole business, with the tasks connected to the money and to the client portal. The focus is the edge, not the feature list.
