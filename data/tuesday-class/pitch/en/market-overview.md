# The freelance market — quick scan (draft, not for publishing)

I wrote this after a week spent buried in reports and a handful of articles. The numbers are mixed from different sources, some of them are my own estimates. Before any of this goes into a deck, every number needs a second check.

## Why this market is interesting

Work is changing. More and more people don't want a manager and don't want an office. They want to work alone, from home, on the thing they actually care about. This isn't a niche anymore. It's a growing slice of the workforce.

We call this category "the one-person company" — somebody who is the CEO, the accountant, the customer support line, and the person who fixes the printer at night, all at once. Those are our customers. Not companies with departments. One human being who is every department.

## Market size (estimates, not precise)

Globally people throw around figures like hundreds of millions of freelancers. I've seen numbers ranging from 1.5 to 1.8 billion people doing some kind of independent work, but that lumps in a huge number of people for whom it isn't the main income, so it's inflated. The slice that's actually relevant to us — people for whom this is the real business, the ones who'll pay for software — is much smaller.

An attempt to cut it into three layers, in annual terms:

- TAM, everyone working independently in the Western world who might pay for a work-management tool. Rough estimate: on the order of 12 to 15 billion dollars a year.
- SAM, the markets we can realistically reach in the next three years — English speakers plus Israel plus Western Europe, freelancers for whom this is the main income: around 2 to 3 billion dollars.
- SOM, what's realistic to capture in the near term with the current team and budget: tens of millions of dollars, say 30 to 60 million a year if everything goes well. That's the ambition for 3 to 4 years out, not for today.

Again, these are estimates. Don't take them as truth.

## Trends working in our favor

The shift to independence is accelerating. The younger generation believes less in one job for life. AI-based tools are lowering the barrier to starting a one-person business, because suddenly one person can do the work of a small team.

On the other hand, most of the tools on the market are still built for teams. They talk about "collaboration" and "assigning tasks to team members." When you're alone, all of that is noise. You don't have a team. That's exactly the gap we fill.

## The risk

This market is price-sensitive. A freelancer checks every shekel. If the product doesn't pay for itself within a month, they cancel. Also, it's easy to enter this category, so competition will grow. Our edge has to be the focus, not the feature list.
