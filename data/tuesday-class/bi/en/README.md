# Practice Kit — BI Market Analysis at TUESDAY (for Ben only)

Internal map. It does not go into the student's download package unless you want it to. Everything else in the folder does.

## What this is
A messy pile of input from a fictional BI department at the company TUESDAY. In class, the student attaches all the files, runs the Point agent that filters and interviews, and then POWER, which produces a presentation. The decision the presentation settles: should the product keep being developed, and specifically "Tasks 2.0," or should it stop.

The mess is a pedagogical goal. Most of the files are real signal, a minority are noise. The student is meant to watch the agent filter.

## What is signal — eight files
- decision-brief.md — frames the question, does not answer it.
- usage-analytics.csv — "Tasks 2.0" adoption rises, but weekly use of it peaks in January and then falls; DAU/MAU is flat-to-declining.
- churn-cohort.md — churn climbs from January; the people who tried the feature and abandoned it churn from the product the most; "too complicated" keeps recurring.
- customer-survey.md — NPS dropped from 31 to 22; "Tasks 2.0" is the number one complaint; the real demand is simplicity and the customer portal.
- competitor-moves.md — Monday and Notion are entering the territory; our differentiator is simplicity, and "Tasks 2.0" pushed us straight into the competitors' strong field.
- market-trend-note.md — the market is growing (supports continuing), but the direction is simplicity and real time savings, not more complexity.
- revenue-pricing-snapshot.md — MRR growth is almost flat; "Tasks 2.0" is a cost center with no direct revenue; two expensive developers and a compute cost that grows with usage.
- tasks2-original-pitch.md — the original marketing promise (simplicity, time savings), which collides head-on with what customers report.

## What is noise — three files
- office-lease-memo.md — office lease renewal.
- vendor-invoice-coffee.md — a coffee invoice.
- recruiting-email.md — a recruiting email about an interview round.

A good Point output will identify these three as irrelevant and leave them out, and briefly explain why.

## The tension in the data — why there is a real argument here
Arguments for continuing: the market is growing, adoption of "Tasks 2.0" climbed throughout the whole period, and the people who never touched it at all churn more.

Arguments for stopping: weekly use of the feature fades after the peak, NPS is dropping, "Tasks 2.0" is the number one complaint and a churn source among the people it disappointed, it is a cost center with no revenue, and the direction the market and the customers want is simplicity and a customer portal — not more complexity.

A reasonable conclusion (not the only one) that a good output will lead to: stop the investment in "Tasks 2.0" and redirect the two developers to the customer portal and to simplicity, while keeping the product itself. But the presentation has to lean on the data, and a student who reaches a different conclusion with reasoning from the data is also right. That is exactly the point.

## What to check when reviewing a student's output
- Did it filter out the three noise files and explain why.
- Was Point interviewed before it built a structure, rather than jumping straight to the presentation.
- Does every slide lean on a document or a number from the folder, with no invented numbers.
- Does the presentation present both sides and lead to a reasoned decision, rather than leaning on opinion.
